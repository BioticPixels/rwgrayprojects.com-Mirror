The 120 Octahedron Polyhedron

The "usual" 120 Polyhedron, or, the one we have labeled as "the" 120 Polyhedron, is essentially the rhombic Triacontahedron with its face centers pushed out.

The 120 Polyhedron and the rhombic triacontahedron

This was done to accommodate 5 Octahedra. (An Octahedron has 6 vertices. So, 5 Octahedra will have 6x5=30 vertices, one for each of the 30 faces of the rhombic Triacontahedron.)

However, the complex of 5 intersecting Octahedra by itself has 120 trianglular faces. Therefore, it is another candidate for Lynnclaire Dennis' "120 Polyhedron".

Here is the build-up of the 5 Octahedra.

As you can see, there are 4 triangular faces around each vertex. So there are 4x30=120 triangular faces.

Each of the 5 Octahedra can be considered to be a Jitterbug.

Lynnclaire has said that suring the dynamic motion of the 120 Polyhedron, there is energy expelled through opennings that form in the polyhedron. The energy is emitted through one type of vertex and is taken back in through another type of vertex. This is exactly the kind of phenomena we see here. As the triangles of the Jitterbug rotate and move radially, their vertices seem to emerge through the Icosahedron-type vertices, follow a curved path, and sbumerge into Dodecahedron-type vertices.
